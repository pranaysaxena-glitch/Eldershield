The Problem

Falls Are the #1 Cause of Injury Death in the Elderly

Every year, millions of elderly individuals fall and cannot get up. Without a way to call for help, they may lie on the floor for hours — dramatically worsening outcomes, causing hypothermia, dehydration, and secondary injuries.

Commercial solutions are expensive, require Wi-Fi, or depend on subscriptions that most families in India simply cannot afford.

  • Elderly individuals living alone cannot reach a phone after a fall
  • A 1-hour delay in response dramatically increases complications
  • Commercial medical alert systems cost ₹5,000–₹20,000+
  • Most require Wi-Fi or active smartphone pairing — impractical for rural areas
  • No affordable, standalone, cellular-based solution exists at scale

GuardianAlert is purpose-built for simplicity, reliability, and real-world daily use.

Two-Stage Detection

No Internet Required

🔋

Rechargeable Wearable

Powered by an 18650 Li-ion cell with TP4056 USB charging. Up to 7 hours of continuous monitoring. Charges overnight like a phone — clip it on and go.

⚡

Instant Dual Alert

On fall detection, the local buzzer activates immediately for on-site awareness while the SIM800L simultaneously dispatches an SMS to the family contact.

🛡️

False Alarm Guard

The two-stage algorithm rejects rapid movements that aren't falls. A 30-second cooldown after each alert prevents SMS spam during recovery or multiple triggers.

🔧

Fully Customisable

How It Works

Six Steps from Fall
to Family Alert

The two-stage detection algorithm is the heart of GuardianAlert. Here's exactly what happens from the moment the device senses movement to the moment an SMS lands on your phone.

1
Sense

Continuous Acceleration Monitoring

The MPU6050 reads 3-axis acceleration (X, Y, Z) and angular velocity 10 times per second via I²C. Data streams to the Arduino Nano continuously, 24/7, with zero user interaction required.

2
Calculate

Magnitude Computation

Arduino computes the Euclidean magnitude of the 3D acceleration vector every 100ms: a = √(ax² + ay² + az²). Normal walking stays around 1.0–1.5g. This single number represents total force on the body regardless of device orientation.

3
Detect

Impact Spike Detection

When the computed magnitude crosses 2.5g, the system enters a fall watch window. This threshold is calibrated to catch the sharp acceleration spike that occurs when a body hits the ground, while ignoring smaller jolts from everyday activities.

4
Confirm

Stillness Confirmation — The Key Innovation

After 500ms, the system re-samples. If acceleration is now below 0.3g (person lying still), the fall is confirmed. If the person is still moving (jumped, sat down fast), no alert fires. This is what separates GuardianAlert from single-threshold systems.

5
Alert

Simultaneous Dual Alert

Both alerts fire at the same instant: the buzzer activates locally at 1kHz for 10 seconds providing on-site audio warning, while the SIM800L transmits an SMS to the preset emergency contact. SMS delivery takes 10–15 seconds on most networks.

6
Cooldown

30-Second Cooldown Period

After an alert, the system waits 30 seconds before re-entering detection. This prevents duplicate SMS messages if the person moves during recovery, while ensuring the system is monitoring again quickly enough to catch a second event.

Arduino — Core Detection Logic
void loop() {
  // Sample every 100ms
  float a = getAccelMagnitude();
  Serial.println(a);

  // Stage 1: Impact spike
  if (a > 2.5) {
    delay(500); // wait for fall

    // Stage 2: Stillness check
    float a2 = getAccelMagnitude();
    if (a2 < 0.3) {
      // Fall confirmed!
      triggerAlert();
      delay(30000); // cooldown
    }
  }
  delay(100);
}

float getAccelMagnitude() {
  int16_t ax, ay, az;
  mpu.getAcceleration(&ax,&ay,&az);
  float x = ax / 16384.0;
  float y = ay / 16384.0;
  float z = az / 16384.0;
  return sqrt(x*x + y*y + z*z);
}

void triggerAlert() {
  tone(BUZZ_PIN, 1000);
  digitalWrite(LED_PIN, HIGH);
  sendSMS("+91XXXXXXXXXX",
    "FALL DETECTED!");
}

Testing & Calibration

Verify Your Build
in 6 Steps

Open the Arduino Serial Monitor at 9600 baud before starting. You'll see live g-force values printed every 100ms to help with calibration.

1

Power On — Serial Monitor Check

Upload the code, open Serial Monitor at 9600 baud. You should see "GSM ready." then "System ready." printed. If neither appears, check your wiring.

Expected Output

GSM ready. → System ready. → g-force values streaming every 100ms

2

Baseline Reading — Standing Still

Hold the device completely still and observe Serial Monitor values. Earth's gravity alone should produce approximately 1.0g at all times regardless of device orientation.

Expected Output

Steady readings between 0.95g and 1.05g — this confirms the MPU6050 is working correctly

3

Walk Test — No False Alarms

Clip the device to your belt and walk normally, sit down, and stand up. Monitor the Serial Monitor. None of these activities should trigger an alert.

Expected Output

Values fluctuate 0.8g – 1.8g. No "Fall Confirmed" message and no SMS sent

4

Simulated Fall — Drop Test

Hold the device in your hand at chest height, then drop it sharply downward onto a padded surface (folded cloth or cushion). The impact spike should exceed 2.5g.

Expected Output

"Impact spike detected!" → "Fall CONFIRMED — Alerting..." → SMS arrives in 10–15 seconds

5

GSM Network Check — SIM LED

Observe the small LED on the SIM800L module after power-on. The blink pattern tells you the network registration status before attempting any SMS tests.

LED Patterns

Blink every 3s = registered on network ✓  |  Fast blink = still searching  |  No blink = not powered

6

Threshold Calibration

Use Serial Monitor readings to fine-tune FALL_THRESHOLD. If false alarms occur during vigorous activity, raise the value. If real falls aren't detected, lower it gradually by 0.2g increments.

Calibration Guide

Walk: 1.0–1.5g  |  Sit fast: 1.5–2.0g  |  Fall: 2.5g+  |  Adjust threshold between these ranges

FAQ

Frequently Asked Questions

GuardianAlert works on any network that supports the SIM800L quad-band GSM module. In India this includes Airtel, Jio, Vi (Vodafone-Idea), and BSNL. You need a standard active prepaid SIM card with SMS capability — no data plan required. Any nano-SIM with a simple SMS pack will work.

Open the Arduino code and find the line: #define EMERGENCY_NUMBER "+91XXXXXXXXXX". Replace the X digits with the actual 10-digit mobile number including the +91 country code prefix. Re-upload the sketch to the Arduino Nano. You can store multiple numbers by calling sendSMS() multiple times inside triggerAlert().

The two-stage detection algorithm is specifically designed to minimise false alarms. If you're still experiencing them, open the Serial Monitor, observe the g-force readings during the activity that's triggering false alarms, and raise the FALL_THRESHOLD by 0.2–0.3g increments until false triggers stop. The algorithm will not confirm a fall unless both the impact spike AND the subsequent stillness condition are met.

On a fully-charged Samsung or LG 18650 cell (~2600mAh), the system runs for approximately 6–8 hours of continuous monitoring. The SIM800L is the biggest power draw. Charging via the TP4056 USB module takes about 2–3 hours. A practical daily routine is to charge the device overnight and wear it during waking hours — similar to a smartwatch.

Yes, but accuracy varies by position. The best placement is the belt or waist — it's closest to the body's centre of mass and gives the most stable, representative acceleration readings. A shirt or trouser pocket also works reasonably well. Avoid wrist placement as frequent hand movements cause false alarms. The algorithm uses magnitude rather than orientation, so exact orientation doesn't matter as long as placement is consistent.

About the Project

Made in India.
Built for India.

GuardianAlert was born from a simple observation: the people who need fall detection the most — elderly individuals in small towns and villages — are the least able to afford commercial solutions.

By building on accessible Arduino hardware and using the existing cellular network rather than Wi-Fi, this project delivers life-saving technology at a fraction of the cost of commercial alternatives — with components available at any local electronics market.

This is an open-source mini IoT project designed for engineering students, hobbyists, and families who want to build their own safety devices without compromise.
